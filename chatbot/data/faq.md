# Frequently Asked Questions

**What is your preferred development stack?**
My preferred stack consists of Python, LangChain, FastAPI, PyTorch, React, Streamlit, and the Hugging Face Hub. I use React for production UI design, Streamlit for rapid machine learning prototyping, and FastAPI to build modular, clean endpoints. Additionally, I leverage developer tools like the Antigravity CLI to speed up my coding and system integration workflows.

**How do you handle GPU/VRAM hardware constraints when training models?**
As an M.Tech student, local computational power is often a bottleneck. I bypass local hardware memory limits by training my deep learning models in cloud compute environments, specifically leveraging Google Colab and Kaggle. For larger generative AI applications, I combine these environments with API-based inference pipelines, allowing me to build robust applications without being constrained by local hardware limitations.

**What makes you a strong candidate for an AI/ML or Systems Engineering internship?**
I do not just import a model and call `.fit()`. I focus on architecting end-to-end data and machine learning pipelines, ensuring that data ingestion, preprocessing, training, and deployment are structured and modular. More importantly, I plan extensively before writing code: I spend time fully understanding the core business or research problem, mapping out the architecture, and then executing the implementation.

**How do you approach model failures and performance optimization?**
In real-world machine learning, nearly 80% of models fail to make it to production. When a model's accuracy or generalizability is poor, I rely on robust validation techniques (such as K-Fold Cross-Validation) and Ensemble Learning (bagging, boosting, and stacking) to build stable predictors. I also use hyperparameter optimization frameworks like `Optuna` to systematically search for the best model parameters rather than guessing.

**What is your communication and leadership style, based on your TA experience?**
Guiding 80+ students as a Teaching Assistant has taught me to be an active listener. When a student brings a coding block, I first focus on understanding their logic and what they are trying to achieve, rather than immediately writing a fix. I outline the resolution path, guide them through it, and invite them to ask follow-up questions. During assessment vivas, if a student does not know the answer, I always take the opportunity to explain the correct answer to them. I believe evaluations should be learning experiences, not just grading checkpoints.
